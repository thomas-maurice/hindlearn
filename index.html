Primer — five things English speakers need to know

1. Aspirated vs plain

Many consonants come in pairs: a plain one and an aspirated one with a little puff of air. In English, the p in "pat" is aspirated, the one in "spat" is not — but English doesn't care about the difference. Hindi does: पल (pal, "moment") and फल (phal, "fruit") are completely different words. Aspirated letters are written with an extra h: kha, gha, cha, chha, jha, Tha, Dha, tha, dha, pha, bha.

2. Voiced vs unvoiced

Unvoiced = vocal cords silent (k, t, p). Voiced = vocal cords humming (g, d, b). Every consonant row pairs them up.

3. Retroflex vs dental (the "two T's" trap)

Hindi has two T's and two D's:

  • Dental (त, द) — tongue touches the back of your upper teeth. Softer than English, closer to Spanish or French t/d.
  • Retroflex (ट, ड) — tongue curled back, tip on the roof of your mouth. Harder and drier. Capitalized in transliteration: Ta, Da.

English t/d sit roughly between the two — which is why native English speakers confuse them.

4. Long vs short vowels

Vowel length changes the word. इ (i) is short like "sit", ई (ii) is long like "see". Hold the long ones about twice as long.

5. The varga (the consonant table)

Consonants are organized by where they're made in the mouth: back → front. Five rows of five, each row: unvoiced / unvoiced+aspirated / voiced / voiced+aspirated / nasal. Once you see the pattern you don't need to memorize 25 random letters — you memorize one table.
